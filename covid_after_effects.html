Most Common 'After-Effects' Of COVID-19 Post Recovery


COVID-19 carries long term side-effects

As infection cases continue to soar nationwide, the evidence is becoming clear that COVID is simply not just a cold or flu-like infection. It poses risk for people across all age groups, in different degrees and affects some more gravely than the others.For many, viral load may have depleted but some typical symptoms, such as cough, itching throat and fatigue may continue to persist for weeks after combatting the illness.

Fatigue

It can be tough to get back on your feet post-recovery. With COVID, long-lasting fatigue, weakness (which could persist for weeks and months) has been a much-feared consequence. This occurs after COVID. Most people will get better quickly but some will find their fatigue persists for three to six months. Post-recovery fatigue has also been recorded in the past outbreaks of infections like the SARS and Epstein-Barr virus.

Breathlessness

Breathlessness and tugging chest pain could be some of the primary symptoms which could pinpoint how bad your COVID infection is. However, for some patients who have made a recovery from the infection, breathlessness might persist for longer and throw them out of spin. It usually improves as the patient regains her fitness, but some people will benefit from assessment by their Doctor or specialist team.

Memory and cognitive decline

As per the study's findings, one-quarter of the patients who had either moderate or severe form of COVID admitted to suffering from neuropsychological deficits and related symptoms. Symptoms likened to PTSD post-treatment were also observed. Mental distress, problems in memory comprehension or behaviour could be triggered due to a variety of factors occurring both inside and outside the recovery centre.

Stress and anxiety

The lockdown has brought upon unprecedented stress to our lives but for those battling the disease, or who have made a recovery, stress and anxiety could be much harder to combat, making them more likely to suffer from psychological ailments in the long run.

Degrading quality of life

Nearly 69% of the patients put under intensive care and 49% of those with mild to moderate infections admitted to suffering from problems in rehabilitating back to their lives. There have been links to the multitude of ways the virus could impact one's lung, heart, nervous system and leave deficits in other vital functioning. A sharp decline and degradation in the quality of life were one of the most commonly observed symptoms in a patient's post-treatment.
